Design Of Two-stage CMOS Operational Amplifier in 180nm Technology With Low Power

 

Abstract—This paper presents the design and analysis of a high-gain, low-power, two-stage CMOS operational amplifier (op-amp). Op-amp topologies, such as folded cascade and two-stage are discussed in this paper. The theoretical and topological analyses of each design are highlighted in detail, including the trade-off among various parameters such as gain, noise, output swings and power consumption.

Keywords—ADC, CMOS amplifier, two-stage amplifier. (key words)

I. Introduction

Due to the growing demand for smaller dimensions and longer battery life for portable applications in all market sectors including telecommunications, medical, computing and consumer electronics, the trend towards low voltage and low power silicon chip systems has increased. Operational amplifiers (Op-amp) are undoubtedly one of the most useful devices in analog electronic circuits. Operational amplifiers vary in complexity and can be used to implement functions ranging from simply generating a DC bias to high speed amplification or filtering. It can perform various analog signal processing tasks with just a few external components. Operational amplifiers are one of the most widely used electronic devices today, widely used in consumer, industrial, and scientific devices. Operational amplifiers, commonly called Op-amps, are one of the most common components in analog electronic circuits.

The analogue-to-digital converter (ADC) is an important building block in mixed signal applications. This circuit plays a significant role in ensuring the analogue signal is accurately represented in the digital domain. For this circuit, an operational amplifier (Op-amp) plays a significant role, since it determines the linearity and noise performance of the ADC.

Op-amps are linear devices that not only have almost all of the properties required for ideal DC amplification, but are also widely used in signal conditioning, filtering, and performing math operations (such as addition, subtraction, integration, differentiation, etc.). Operational amplifiers are generally three-port devices. In the symbol of an operational amplifier it mainly consists of an inverting input, represented by a negative sign ("-"), and another non-inverting input, represented by a positive sign ("+"). Both inputs are very high impedance. The output signal of the operational amplifier is the amplified difference between the two input signals, i.e. the amplified differential input. In general, the input stage of an operational amplifier is usually a differential amplifier.

Our goal is to develop a physical design and make a low power operational amplifier. An ideal operational amplifier with a single-ended output is characterized by a differential input, an infinite voltage gain, an infinite input resistance and an output resistance of zero. However, these characters cannot be produced in an actual operational amplifier, but their performance must be good enough to bring the circuit performance close to the characters of an ideal operational amplifier in most applications. With the introduction of a new generation of CMOS technology, the design of operational amplifiers continues to face additional challenges due to the reduction in supply voltage and transistor channel length.
